Ask your own AI assistant how bussetech.com can help your business, and this is the page it should read. The three sections answer one question each — can the studio build the thing you need, and where is the proof.

## Information products

What it proves: the studio can found and maintain a live, source-cited open dataset, and the product gnomes that build one generalize to the next domain without a rewrite.

KDC is the anchor: a live open dataset tracking United States data-center projects, founded in a day and sustained by dispatched agent cycles, with every run in the cost ledger and every change arriving as a pull request. The same pair of information gnomes, with named ancestors on the leaderboard, now runs two further domains. Menowise aggregates menopause-health evidence; Backpacks aggregates technical-carry maker specifications. Both are live and seeding their datasets; KDC is the fully populated one. Three builds, three domains, one shared product.

The studio's SaaS work is client engagement carried under its privacy controls (ADR-0022-class: no client, no participants, no figures appear in public). Its proof tile publishes here the moment the client consent gate clears — until then, this page makes no claim about it.

## Clean-room rebuilds

What it proves: the studio can take a legacy system, decompose its behavior into requirements, and rebuild it cleanly on modern architecture with provenance that never touches the original source.

Lemonade Stand is the most demonstrable of these: a live, playable clean-room rebuild of the classic business simulation, run by its own gnome shift. Open it and play.

GenMURK is a clean-room rebuild of a classic multi-user text world. Its living-documentation site works in public: the decomposition, the requirements, the architecture decisions, and the provenance are all readable as the rebuild proceeds. The heritage systems that inform it are shown as lineage, not as the product; the running world itself is scheduled for a later cycle, so this page claims the plan and the public build log, not a live game. Behind both sits a reusable intake capability that surveys a legacy codebase (its languages, its licenses, its shape) before any rebuild begins, proven on the same text-world survey.

A third rebuild, Robot Odyssey, is named here as a deliberately gated capstone: it waits behind a rights question and nothing has been built. Three rebuilds, approached three ways, is the point of the slate.
